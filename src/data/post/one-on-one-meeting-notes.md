## What are one on one meeting notes? {#what-are-one-on-one-notes}

One on one meeting notes are a private, ongoing document that tracks a single recurring conversation between a manager and one direct report over time. They are not a transcript. They're a working memory of commitments and context that would otherwise get lost between meetings.

The format is different from team meeting minutes in one important way: continuity. A board meeting produces a standalone document. One on one notes are the same document (or the same running thread) reused week after week, so both people can scroll back and see what was said a month or a year ago. That history is the entire point. Without it, every 1:1 starts from zero and the employee has to re-explain context they already gave you.

According to the [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2022/11/make-the-most-of-your-one-on-one-meetings) piece by management researcher Steven Rogelberg, published in November 2022, few organizations give managers real guidance on how to run these meetings well, and poorly run one on ones leave employees feeling disconnected rather than supported. A shared notes document is one of the simplest fixes. It forces structure without forcing formality.

## What should a one on one agenda include? {#agenda}

A one on one agenda should include four sections: a quick check-in, current priorities and blockers, career or feedback discussion, and a short list of action items from both sides. Each section gets a few minutes, not a full page.

Here's what each section is actually for.

**Check-in (2-5 minutes).** Not "how are you" as filler. This is where you catch a burnout signal or a personal issue before it becomes a performance problem. Skip it and you'll miss things that matter.

**Priorities and blockers (10-15 minutes).** What's the employee working on, and what's in their way. This is where most managers default to status-update mode, which wastes the meeting. If the work is already visible in a project tool, don't re-narrate it here. Talk about what's stuck.

**Career and feedback (5-10 minutes).** Not every week needs a deep career conversation, but it should come up regularly, not just at review time. This is also where constructive feedback belongs, given with a specific example rather than a general impression.

**Action items (2-3 minutes).** What did each of you just commit to. Write it down before the meeting ends. If it's not written down, it usually doesn't happen.

| Agenda section | Time | Who drives it |
| --------------- | ---- | -------------- |
| Check-in | 2-5 min | Either person |
| Priorities and blockers | 10-15 min | Employee |
| Career and feedback | 5-10 min | Both |
| Action items | 2-3 min | Manager captures, both own |

I've sat through 1:1s that were nothing but status updates read off a task board, and they're a waste of two people's time. The value of a one on one isn't the update. It's the ten minutes where something the employee wouldn't put in a Slack message finally comes up.

## What is the best one on one template? {#template}

The best one on one template is a single reusable document with a header, a running log by date, and a persistent action items table, so both people can scroll back through history instead of starting fresh each time. Simplicity beats structure here.

Copy this. It works as a shared Google Doc, a Notion page, or plain text.

```
ONE ON ONE: [Manager name] & [Employee name]
Recurring: [day/time] · [30 min]

--- [Date] ---

Check-in:
-

Priorities / blockers:
-

Career / feedback:
-

Action items:
- [ ] [Task] (Owner: [name], due: [date])
- [ ] [Task] (Owner: [name], due: [date])

Carried over from last time:
-
```

Three things make this template work better than a generic notes page.

**1. It's one continuous document, not a new file each week.** Scrolling up to see what came up a month ago takes five seconds. Searching through separate files takes five minutes, and most people never bother.

**2. Action items carry forward automatically.** Anything unfinished from last time gets copied to the top of the new entry. Nothing quietly disappears just because the meeting ended.

**3. It stays under one page per entry.** If your notes for a single 1:1 run longer than that, you're documenting the conversation instead of having it.

## What questions should you ask in a one on one meeting? {#questions}

The best one on one questions fall into four categories: how the person is doing, what's blocking their work, where they want their career to go, and what feedback they have for you as their manager. Rotate through categories rather than asking all four every week.

**How they're doing**

- What's your energy like this week, on a scale of 1 to 5?
- Is anything outside work affecting how things are going here?
- What's the best part of your week been so far?

**Priorities and blockers**

- What's the one thing that would make your week easier if it went away?
- Is there anything you're stuck on that you haven't raised yet?
- Do you have what you need to hit your current goals?

**Career and growth**

- What's a skill you'd like to build in the next few months?
- Where do you want to be in a year, and is your current work moving you there?
- Is there a project you'd want to be closer to?

**Feedback, in both directions**

- Is there something I did recently that helped or got in your way?
- What's one thing I could do differently as your manager?
- Did the feedback from our last conversation land the way I meant it?

Quantum Workplace's research on one on one meetings notes that a clear agenda and specific questions consistently outperform open-ended "so, how's it going" check-ins, because vague questions get vague answers. Ask something concrete and you'll get something concrete back.

Don't run all twelve questions in a single meeting. Pick two or three, let the conversation breathe, and come back to the rest another week.

## How often should you hold one on one meetings? {#frequency}

Weekly 30-minute one on ones are the most common recommendation, though experienced employees can move to biweekly once the working relationship is established. Consistency matters more than exact frequency.

Gallup's 2024 workplace research found that employees who meet regularly with their manager are three times as likely to be engaged, and teams where managers hold consistent one on ones are 1.5 times more likely to have every member retained. Compare that to teams without a regular cadence, where engagement drops sharply and turnover risk climbs.

| Frequency | Best for | Risk if skipped |
| --------- | -------- | ---------------- |
| Weekly | New hires, high-change projects | Context gets lost fast |
| Biweekly | Established, experienced team members | Still workable if agenda is tight |
| Monthly | Senior ICs with high autonomy | Small issues can go unnoticed too long |
| Ad hoc only | Nobody, according to the research | Employees report feeling disconnected |

The frequency question comes up constantly, but the research is fairly settled on one point: one-off one on ones don't move the needle. The pattern matters more than any single conversation. Cancel a 1:1 three weeks running and the employee reads it as "not a priority," even if that's not what you meant.

## How do you take one on one notes without losing the conversation? {#taking-notes}

You take one on one notes without losing the conversation by preparing the template before the meeting starts, writing only outcomes and commitments during it, and filling in detail immediately after while it's still fresh. The goal is minimal typing during the actual conversation.

1. **Open last week's entry before the meeting.** Read the carried-over action items so you're not surprised by what's outstanding.
2. **Add today's date and paste the template sections in.** You're filling gaps, not building structure from a blank page.
3. **During the meeting, jot fragments, not sentences.** "Skill: wants PM track" is enough. You'll expand it after.
4. **Write down every commitment the second it's made.** If the employee says "I'll send that draft Thursday," it goes in the action items table immediately, not from memory later.
5. **Spend two minutes right after the meeting cleaning up your fragments.** This is the step most people skip, and it's the one that makes the notes actually useful next time.

The honest tradeoff: if you're typing full sentences during the meeting, you're not fully listening. I'd rather have messy fragments and a real conversation than a clean transcript and a distracted employee across the table.

## Should you use AI to take one on one meeting notes? {#ai-notes}

Yes, if it saves you from choosing between listening and typing, but the right AI tool for one on ones depends on how sensitive the conversation gets. 1:1s regularly cover compensation, personal circumstances, and performance concerns, which raises the privacy stakes higher than a team standup.

Here's how the main approaches stack up:

| Approach | Attention cost | Privacy | Works for sensitive topics |
| -------- | --------------- | ------- | ---------------------------- |
| Manual notes during the meeting | High | Full | Yes |
| Cloud AI (Otter, Fireflies, Fathom) | Low | Audio uploaded to vendor servers | Risky for HR-sensitive topics |
| Local AI (Siplinx AI) | Low | Nothing leaves the device | Yes |

Cloud transcription tools record the conversation and process it on remote servers. For a sales call or a product review, that's a reasonable trade. ## Key takeaways

- One on one notes are a continuous document, not a one-off summary. History across weeks is the whole point.
- A working template has four sections: check-in, priorities and blockers, career and feedback, and action items, kept to one page per entry.
- Gallup (2024) found regular one on ones triple the odds an employee is engaged at work.
- Let the employee drive most of the agenda. HBR's research names this the strongest predictor of a useful 1:1.

## FAQ {#faq}

**What should be included in one on one meeting notes?**
A check-in note, current priorities or blockers the employee raises, any career or feedback discussion, and an action items table listing what each person committed to and by when. Carry unfinished items forward to the next entry.

**What is a good one on one template?**
A single running document per employee, reused every week, with a short header, a dated log entry per meeting, and a persistent action items list. Avoid creating a new file for every session; continuity is what makes the notes useful.

**Who should set the one on one agenda?**
The employee should drive most of it. Research from Harvard Business Review found that agendas set collaboratively, with the employee contributing the majority of topics, are the strongest predictor of a productive meeting.

**How long should a one on one meeting be?**
Thirty minutes is the most common length and correlates with the highest engagement in workplace research. Fifteen minutes works for a quick sync with a senior team member; an hour is reasonable for a new hire in their first month.

**What questions should a manager ask in a one on one?**
Rotate between four types: how the person is doing, what's blocking their current work, where they want to grow, and what feedback they have for you. Two or three good questions per meeting beat a long checklist.

**Should one on one notes be shared with the employee?**
Yes. A one on one is a two-way conversation, and the notes should live somewhere both people can see and edit, not in the manager's private file. Shared notes also make it obvious when an action item gets dropped.

**Can AI take notes during a one on one meeting?**
Yes. Tools like Otter.ai, Fireflies.ai, and Siplinx AI can transcribe and summarize a one on one automatically. Cloud tools send the audio to their servers for processing.
