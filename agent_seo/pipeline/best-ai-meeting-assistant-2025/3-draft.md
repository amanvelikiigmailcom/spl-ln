## What is an AI meeting assistant and how does it work?

An AI meeting assistant is software that records your meeting audio, converts speech to text, generates a summary, and pulls out action items — with no manual note-taking required. Most tools work in one of two ways: a bot joins your Zoom or Teams call as a participant, or a desktop app listens to your system audio directly.

The bot-based model is the most common. Tools like Fireflies.ai, Otter.ai, Fathom, and tl;dv send a virtual participant into your call. That participant records audio and streams it to cloud servers for processing. You get a transcript and summary within minutes of the meeting ending.

The desktop model works differently. The app runs locally on your computer, captures audio through your microphone or system output, and processes it either on-device or by sending audio to the cloud. [Siplinx AI](https://siplinx.com/?utm_source=own_blog&utm_medium=article&utm_campaign=best-ai-meeting-assistant-2025&utm_content=hero-definition) uses this model but goes further: it processes everything with a local LLM and local speech-to-text engine, so no audio or transcript ever leaves your machine.

Why does the processing model matter? According to a Metrigy 2024 study, 49.2% of employees already have a license for an AI virtual assistant. But adoption has moved faster than legal frameworks. That gap is exactly where the lawsuits of 2025 were born.

## Why AI meeting assistants became a legal risk in 2025

This is the section every competitor article skips. And it's the most important thing to know before you pick a tool.

In August 2025, a class-action lawsuit was filed against Otter.ai in California federal court. The core allegations: OtterPilot, Otter's auto-joining bot, records meetings without obtaining consent from all participants. Recordings were allegedly used to train Otter's AI models without explicit permission from the people whose voices were recorded. [NPR covered the filing in detail.](https://www.npr.org/2025/08/15/g-s1-83087/otter-ai-transcription-class-action-lawsuit)

One user's account, surfaced by the NPR investigation, was direct: Otter had recorded a Zoom meeting with investors, then shared a transcription that included confidential details about a business discussed after the user had already left the call.

Fireflies.ai faced its own legal challenge. In December 2025, Illinois resident Katelin Cruz filed a complaint — Cruz v. Fireflies.AI Corp., No. 3:25-cv-03399 — under Illinois's Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA). The complaint alleged that Fireflies.ai's meeting bot records and analyzes the unique vocal characteristics of every meeting participant, including people who never created a Fireflies account and never agreed to its terms. [The Workplace Privacy Report published the full case analysis in April 2026.](https://www.workplaceprivacyreport.com/2026/04/articles/artificial-intelligence/ai-meeting-assistants-and-biometric-privacy-governance-lessons-from-the-fireflies-ai-lawsuit/)

Damages sought: $1,000 per negligent violation and $5,000 per reckless or intentional violation.

These lawsuits point to a structural problem in how most AI meeting tools are built. The bot-join model means your tool is collecting audio from people who didn't sign up for anything. For legal teams running strategy calls, HR conducting investigation interviews, healthcare providers discussing patient care, and executives reviewing sensitive financials, this is not a theoretical concern. It's a liability.

## Which assistant is best for privacy-sensitive professions

The right tool depends heavily on what you discuss in meetings.

**For lawyers:** Attorney-client privilege creates a specific problem with cloud-based meeting tools. Any audio that leaves your machine and lives on a third-party server is potentially discoverable or subject to breach. The ABA's Model Rules don't specify a product, but they do require reasonable steps to protect client confidentiality. A cloud tool that trains on your recordings is the opposite of reasonable steps. Local processing tools like [Siplinx AI](https://siplinx.com/privacy/?utm_source=own_blog&utm_medium=article&utm_campaign=best-ai-meeting-assistant-2025&utm_content=lawyers-privacy-section) eliminate that risk by design.

**For healthcare professionals:** HIPAA prohibits sharing protected health information (PHI) with vendors who aren't covered by a Business Associate Agreement (BAA). Most AI meeting tools offer BAAs only on enterprise plans, and even then you're trusting a cloud vendor's security practices. Local processing means PHI never leaves the room.

**For executives handling M&A or investor discussions:** The Otter.ai case is instructive. Confidential deal discussions were shared in transcripts with people who had left the meeting. That's not a feature — it's a data handling failure. Tools that process audio in the cloud have to store it somewhere, access it for training, and manage retention. That's three new attack surfaces on your deal information.

**For finance and banking:** FINRA and SEC rules create additional complexity around recording and storing client communications. A tool that stores recordings in a third-party cloud adds a compliance step that local processing avoids.

The short answer: if your meetings include information you'd classify as confidential under any regulatory framework, the only category that removes third-party data risk entirely is local AI meeting assistants.

## What makes an AI meeting assistant safe for confidential conversations

Four questions tell you most of what you need to know before choosing a tool:

**1. Where does the audio go?** If it leaves your device, it lives on someone else's server. Ask for the vendor's data residency documentation, not just their privacy page.

**2. Is your data used to train models?** Otter.ai's lawsuit centers on exactly this. Some tools opt you out by default; others opt you in. Read the terms, not the marketing copy.

**3. Does the bot join as a meeting participant?** Bot-join tools create a social dynamic problem (participants can see the bot) and a consent problem (participants who didn't consent to recording). Bot-free tools that capture system audio are meaningfully different.

**4. What's the retention policy?** Some tools keep your transcripts indefinitely unless you manually delete them. Others offer configurable retention or automatic deletion. For regulated industries, data retention policies need to match your compliance framework.

The Fireflies.ai BIPA lawsuit specifically cited three failures: no published retention/destruction policy, no written notice to participants, and no consent from non-account-holders. Those three failures are a checklist for what to verify before you buy any tool.

## How to set up a private AI meeting assistant

These steps apply specifically to setting up an offline, local AI meeting assistant. The setup for cloud tools is simpler (sign up, install browser extension, done) but skips the privacy controls this section covers.

1. **Download and install the app.** For Siplinx AI, download the Mac or Windows desktop app from siplinx.com. No account required — the app runs entirely on your machine.

2. **Choose your local model.** Siplinx AI uses a local LLM for summarization and a local speech-to-text engine for transcription. On first launch, it downloads the model files to your device. This takes a few minutes and requires about 2-4 GB of disk space depending on the model size you choose.

3. **Set your audio source.** In the app settings, select whether to capture system audio (for virtual meetings you're attending on your computer) or microphone input (for in-person meetings). You can also capture both simultaneously.

4. **Test with a short recording.** Run a 2-minute test recording before your first real meeting. Check that transcription accuracy looks right for your voice and accent. Adjust the model if needed.

5. **Join your meeting.** No bot joins the call. You attend as normal. The app runs silently in the background and captures audio from your selected source.

6. **Review your notes.** After the meeting, Siplinx generates a summary, transcript, and action item list — all stored locally. You can export to PDF, copy to your note-taking app, or leave them on-device.

7. **Configure retention.** In settings, decide how long to keep recordings and transcripts. For maximum privacy, set automatic deletion after your review window.

The entire process works without an internet connection after initial setup. That means it works on flights, in secure facilities, or anywhere your IT policy restricts cloud uploads.

## What "free" AI meeting assistants actually cost you

Most tools in this category advertise a free tier. The actual free offering is rarely enough for regular use.

Otter.ai's free plan gives you 300 monthly transcription minutes, which sounds generous until you realize that's five hours of meetings per month. The average knowledge worker sits in [roughly 23 hours of meetings per week](https://hbr.org/2022/03/dear-manager-youre-holding-too-many-meetings), meaning Otter's free tier covers about 13% of your actual need.

Fireflies.ai's free plan limits transcript storage to 800 minutes of meeting history. After that, older transcripts disappear. For anyone who needs to reference past meetings, that's a 0 to 13 month shelf life on your data — unless you pay.

tl;dv's free plan caps you at 10 video recordings per month. It's enough to evaluate the tool, not to run a business on.

The real cost structure looks like this for most professionals:

| Plan tier      | Monthly cost | What it actually limits                                |
| -------------- | ------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Free           | $0           | Recording minutes, storage, integrations, AI summaries |
| Pro/Individual | $15-25/mo    | Some storage caps, limited seats                       |
| Business       | $30-75/mo    | Full features, BAA access, admin controls              |

And there's a less visible cost: your data. Several tools use free-tier recordings to train their models. Paying for a plan doesn't always opt you out — you need to read the enterprise data processing agreement to be certain.

For a solo professional with a light meeting load, the free tier of Fathom or Fireflies is probably adequate. For anyone handling sensitive conversations or needing reliable history, the real cost lands at $20-30/month per user. At that price point, the differences between tools become a real comparison, not a marketing exercise.

## FAQ

**What is the best free AI meeting assistant?**
Fathom offers the most generous free plan for individuals: unlimited recordings and transcripts with no monthly cap, though storage and some AI features require a paid plan. For teams needing HIPAA compliance or high-volume use, free tiers across all major tools have meaningful limitations.

**Are AI meeting assistants safe to use for confidential meetings?**
Cloud-based tools carry risks: audio is processed on third-party servers, data may be used for model training, and participants who didn't consent may be recorded. In 2025-2026, Otter.ai and Fireflies.ai both faced lawsuits over these practices. For confidential meetings, local AI meeting assistants that process everything on-device (like Siplinx AI) eliminate these risks by design.

**Can an AI meeting assistant work without a bot joining the call?**
Yes. Two models exist: bot-join (Fireflies, Otter, Fathom, tl;dv) and bot-free (Granola, Siplinx AI). Bot-free tools capture audio from your system or microphone without sending a visible participant into the call. This avoids the consent issues associated with bots and is less disruptive to meeting dynamics.

**What is the best AI meeting assistant for HIPAA compliance?**
Tools offering HIPAA BAAs include Otter.ai (Business plan), Fireflies.ai (Business plan), and Fellow (Enterprise). The safest option for healthcare settings is a local AI meeting assistant that never transmits audio outside your device — no BAA is needed if there's no third-party data processing.

**How accurate are AI meeting transcriptions?**
Accuracy varies significantly by audio conditions. In clean audio, top tools achieve a 6-8% word error rate (WER), meaning roughly 1 in 14 words may be wrong. In noisy environments, WER can reach 24%, according to Krisp's 2026 benchmark study — about 1 in 4 words. Speaker separation (diarization) accuracy ranges from 85% to 97%.

**Does Otter.ai record without permission?**
A class-action lawsuit filed in August 2025 alleged that Otter.ai's OtterPilot feature joins meetings and records participants without obtaining consent from everyone in the call. Otter.ai disputes the claims. The case is ongoing as of June 2026. The lawsuit also alleged recordings were used to train AI models without explicit participant consent.

**What is the difference between a cloud and offline AI meeting assistant?**
Cloud tools send your audio to remote servers for processing and storage. Offline (local) tools process everything on your own machine using local AI models. The practical difference: cloud tools require internet, can be used by vendor data teams, and carry third-party breach risk. Offline tools work without internet, never share data with the vendor, and are the natural choice for air-gapped environments or regulated industries.
